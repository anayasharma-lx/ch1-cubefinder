Chapter 1 · A Square and A Cube

Successive Differences

Take differences of perfect squares, then differences of those — and watch what becomes constant. Do the same for cubes. The number of steps tells you the dimension.

Watch the gnomon grow: Going from (n−1)² to n² adds an L-shaped ring of cells called a gnomon. The gnomon always has 2n−1 cells — the next consecutive odd number. Take the differences of these gnomons and you get a constant: always 2.

L2 is always 2. The gnomons grow by exactly 2 each time (3→5→7→9…). So the second differences are constant = 2 = 2! Two levels needed because a square is a 2-dimensional shape.
Watch the shell form: Going from (n−1)³ to n³ adds an outer shell of unit cubes — everything on the surface that wasn't there before. Shell sizes are 1, 7, 19, 37… Take differences three times to reach a constant: always 6 = 3!

L3 is always 6 = 3! Three levels of differences are needed because a cube is 3-dimensional. Each level "peels off" one dimension — shell → face-strip → edge, then constant.
The general rule: For nk, exactly k levels of differences are needed to reach a constant, and that constant is always k! Squares need 2 levels (constant = 2). Cubes need 3 levels (constant = 6). This is because each level of differencing reduces the polynomial degree by 1.
